OPEN LETTER

It's Time for AI to Fix Our Computers

An open letter to Apple Inc. and Microsoft Corporation

July 4, 2026 — Published at midnight EDT

Dear Apple and Microsoft,

I'm writing to you not as a developer, not as an IT professional, and not as a tech journalist. I'm writing as a regular person who uses your products every single day — and who, like millions of others, has learned to tolerate a level of friction that we shouldn't have to.

The Problem We've All Normalized

When I pick up my iPad, it just works. When I pick up my phone, it just works. The experience is seamless, reliable, and when something does go wrong, it's rare and usually self-correcting.

But when I sit down at my Mac or my Windows PC — machines that cost more and do more — I accept a completely different standard. The fan spins up for no reason. An app freezes. Something called a "background process" is eating 40% of my CPU. A recent update broke something that was working fine yesterday. I Google the symptom, watch a YouTube tutorial, try a fix I don't fully understand, and hope it works.

We all do this. We've done it for decades. We accept it because we've always accepted it — because the first generation of PC users were hobbyists who enjoyed the troubleshooting process, and the rest of us just inherited their tolerance.

But it's 2026. You are both building the most advanced AI ever shipped to consumers. Apple Intelligence is on Mac. Copilot is on Windows. You have the talent, the access, and the infrastructure to solve this. And yet, neither of you has made system health a priority for your AI assistants.

The Gap Nobody Is Filling

Today, your AI efforts are focused on productivity — writing emails, generating images, summarizing documents. These are valuable. But they don't address the daily friction that erodes the experience of using your products.

Meanwhile, the third-party tools that try to address system health are stuck in the past. They clean junk files. They manage startup items. They clear caches. They are janitors, not doctors. The most advanced among them — like CleanMyMac's Smart Insights or IObit's AI Mode — use AI to decide what to clean. None of them use AI to understand why something is wrong.

And the tools that do understand system health — enterprise IT platforms like ControlUp's AI Assistant — are locked behind enterprise pricing and infrastructure requirements that put them out of reach for the individuals and small businesses who need them most.

The gap is clear: nobody has built an AI that proactively monitors a personal computer, conversationally diagnoses problems when they arise, explains what's happening in plain language, and guides the user through a fix.

Not the third-party cleaners. Not the enterprise platforms. And not you.

What We're Asking For

We're not asking for a new app. We're not asking for a subscription. We're asking you to use the AI you've already built — the AI that's already running on our machines — to do something genuinely useful for the people who use your products every day.

Specifically, we'd love to see your AI assistants evolve to:

  1. Monitor proactively. Learn the normal patterns of our machines — CPU usage, memory pressure, disk activity, network behavior — and flag anomalies before we notice them. "Your Mac has been running hotter than usual since Tuesday. A background process called [X] appears to be the cause. Want me to look into it?"
  2. Diagnose conversationally. When we say "my computer's been slow all week," don't give us a generic troubleshooting checklist. Actually investigate. Check process history, memory trends, recent updates, and come back with a specific answer: "Chrome version 127.0.6533 has a memory leak that's been reported. Here's the evidence from your system. You can update to fix it, or I can limit its memory usage in the meantime."
  3. Explain in plain language. Most people don't know what a "daemon" is. They don't know what "kernel_task" does. They don't know why "windowserver" is using 3GB of RAM. Your AI should translate this into language a normal person can understand — and teach us something in the process, so we get smarter over time instead of more dependent.
  4. Guide, don't just fix. Silently changing system settings is scary. Autonomously "repairing" things without explanation erodes trust. The better approach is to diagnose, explain, and offer a one-click guided fix — with a clear explanation of what will change and why. Let us approve the action. Make us partners in the fix, not bystanders.
  5. Prevent, not just react. After enough data, your AI should start recognizing patterns across machines. "We've noticed that users with your specific hardware configuration and app setup are experiencing this issue after the latest update. Here's a recommended setting change to prevent it." You already have telemetry from millions of devices. Use it to prevent problems before they reach us.

Why You Should Do This (Not a Third Party)

You are the only entities that can do this properly. A third-party app will always be working with limited system access, fighting against your security sandboxing, and guessing at what your OS is doing internally. You have:

  • Native system access — no permission walls, no sandboxing limits
  • Telemetry at scale — anonymized data from millions of devices to identify patterns
  • OS-level integration — the ability to actually fix things, not just suggest fixes
  • Trust — users already trust you with their systems
  • The AI is already there — Copilot and Apple Intelligence are already running. This isn't a new product. It's a new capability for an existing one.

A third party could build a version of this. But it would always be second-best — working at arm's length from the OS, with limited data and limited access. You can build the real thing.

Why Now

The AI is ready. The hardware is ready (your NPUs are more than capable). The user expectation is ready — people already talk to Siri and Copilot and expect them to be helpful. The only thing missing is the decision to make system health a priority for your AI.

Every day that passes, more people are watching YouTube tutorials to fix problems that AI could diagnose in seconds. More small businesses are paying IT consultants for issues that could be prevented with proactive monitoring. More users are tolerating a lower standard of experience than your hardware is capable of delivering.

The gap between "my iPad just works" and "why is my fan spinning" is not a hardware problem. It's a software problem. And it's a software problem that AI is uniquely positioned to solve.

A Final Thought

The original promise of personal computing was that it would be personal — that these machines would adapt to us, not the other way around. We've come a long way, but somewhere along the journey, we accepted that "personal" meant "you personally have to maintain it."

You have the tools to fulfill the original promise. We hope you'll use them.

Millions of us are ready for our computers to just work — the way our phones already do. We're not asking for magic. We're asking you to point the AI you've already built at the problem we all live with every day.

Thank you for listening.

Michael David Green

Tokyo, Japan · July 4, 2026
